The Problem

Slab-on-grade
is broken.

The construction industry has innovated above ground for decades — prefab walls, modular structures, off-site fabrication. But the one thing every building still relies on? A concrete foundation poured on-site, the same way it was done 50 years ago.

That process is slow, expensive, labor-intensive, and an outsized source of construction waste and schedule risk. It's the last bottleneck that no one had solved — until now.

The System

Built like a
structural surfboard.

Plank is the world's first patented non-concrete, lightweight, modular foundation system. Invented by David Warner — with over 40 years of Bay Area construction experience — it applies the same torsion-box engineering principle used in aircraft wings and surfboards to the foundation problem.

A precision-engineered "sandwich" of industry-vetted materials creates a structural system that handles full design loads with no concrete — and arrives at the jobsite ready to set. No pour. No cure time. No mess.

Plank integrates subfloor, insulation, waterproofing, and structural capacity into a single prefabricated product. What used to take 15 days now takes 3.

Our Story

Why couldn't foundations
work like a surfboard?

David Warner spent over 40 years building in the Bay Area as founder of Redhorse Constructors. He watched the industry revolutionize framing, walls, roofing, and MEP systems — but the foundation stayed the same. Concrete poured on-site, cured for weeks, at enormous cost.

During the 2020 pandemic, supply chain delays made an already bad situation worse. David started asking a different question: what if a foundation could be engineered the way an airplane wing is? Load-bearing strength from geometry, not mass.

The answer was a torsion box — a "sandwich" of existing, industry-vetted materials that handles structural loads with no concrete. Lighter. Stronger. Faster. Prefabricated in a controlled environment and set on site in a day.

Today, Plank is the first patented non-concrete foundation system in the world — with UC Berkeley testing, Holmes Engineering approval, and a pilot project at UC Davis to prove it.
